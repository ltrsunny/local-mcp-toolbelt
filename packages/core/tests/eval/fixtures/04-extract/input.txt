Unveiling Latent Attention Patterns in Dynamically Routed Sparse Mixture-of-Experts Models

Dr. Anya Sharma, AI Research Labs, DeepMind, London, UK
Prof. Benjamin Carter, Department of Computer Science, Stanford University, Stanford, CA, USA
Ms. Chloe Davis, Google Brain, Mountain View, CA, USA

Abstract:
The advent of Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures has significantly advanced the scalability and performance of large language models, primarily by enabling sparse activation patterns. While the benefits in terms of computational efficiency and model capacity are well-documented, the internal workings of how attention mechanisms interact with dynamic routing remain a complex and underexplored area. This paper presents a novel framework for analyzing the latent attention patterns within sparse MoE models, specifically focusing on how expert activation decisions influence, and are influenced by, the self-attention layers. Our methodology introduces a new interpretability metric, "Expert-Attribution Divergence (EAD)," which quantifies the discrepancy between a token's attention distribution and the expert(s) it is routed to. Through extensive experimentation on a 1.2 trillion parameter MoE model, we demonstrate that optimal routing strategies lead to a lower EAD, indicating a tighter alignment between attention and expert specialization. We observe that models with lower EAD achieve a 3.1% higher accuracy on downstream tasks compared to baseline MoE models without our optimized routing, while simultaneously reducing inference FLOPs by 22% due to more focused expert engagement. Furthermore, we identify distinct, emergent attention patterns that correlate with specific expert functionalities, providing empirical evidence for specialized processing pathways within the MoE architecture. Our findings suggest that current routing mechanisms, while effective, can be further refined by explicitly incorporating attention-based signals, leading to more efficient and interpretable sparse models.

Introduction:
Large neural networks have revolutionized numerous fields, from natural language processing to computer vision. A key challenge in scaling these models further involves managing their ever-increasing computational demands. Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures, by selectively activating only a subset of their parameters for each input, offer a compelling solution to this scalability bottleneck. This approach allows for models with trillions of parameters to be trained and deployed with reasonable computational budgets, surpassing the performance of densely activated models of comparable active parameter counts. However, the synergistic interplay between the attention mechanisms, which are fundamental to modern transformer architectures, and the dynamic routing decisions within MoE layers is still not fully understood. Previous research has largely focused on optimizing routing algorithms or analyzing the overall performance gains of MoE models [Smith et al., 2023]. Less attention has been paid to the granular interaction where a token's attention distribution across other tokens might reveal insights into *why* a particular expert was chosen, or *how* the chosen expert influences the subsequent attention computation.

The problem intensifies when considering the sparse nature of MoE. Each token is typically routed to only a few experts, meaning the overall model capacity is vast, but the activated path is narrow. This sparsity, while efficient, complicates the interpretability of attention, as different experts might process similar input tokens differently, leading to varied attention patterns. Understanding these internal dynamics is crucial for developing more robust, efficient, and interpretable large-scale models. Our work addresses this gap by proposing a systematic investigation into the co-evolution of attention and expert routing. We hypothesize that a strong correlation between a token's attention context and its assigned expert(s) is indicative of an efficient and specialized processing pathway. This investigation is timely given the increasing complexity of MoE models, where subtle routing inaccuracies can lead to significant performance degradation or inefficient resource utilization. The training cost for such large models often runs into millions of dollars, with a particular MoE experiment costing approximately $3.5 million over two months, highlighting the necessity for even marginal improvements in efficiency and understanding. This paper explores methods to quantify and visualize these attention-expert alignments, ultimately aiming to inform the design of next-generation sparse attention mechanisms and dynamic routing strategies [Wang et al., 2024].

Methodology:
To rigorously evaluate our proposed Expert-Attribution Divergence (EAD) metric and analyze attention patterns, we conducted experiments on a proprietary dataset derived from the C4 dataset, tailored for complex reasoning tasks. Our primary benchmark for evaluation was the Long-Range Understanding (LRU) benchmark, which specifically assesses a model's ability to process and synthesize information across extended input sequences. The MoE model architecture under investigation was a transformer-based model with 64 experts per layer, where each token was routed to 2 experts. We employed a modified top-k routing mechanism, integrating a regularization term based on EAD during training. This regularization encouraged a stronger alignment between the attention weights and the activated experts. We fine-tuned the model for 500,000 steps on 2048 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Performance metrics included perplexity on held-out data, accuracy on LRU, and the total FLOPs per inference step. Our experimental setup ensures a high degree of control over the routing and attention mechanisms, allowing for a detailed examination of their interactions. Previous works have shown promise in understanding individual components [Johnson et al., 2024], but a holistic approach combining attention and routing has been lacking until now. Our approach aims to bridge this gap, offering a comprehensive view of how these critical elements contribute to the overall model behavior. The total active parameters for our model were roughly 130 billion, showcasing a significant yet manageable scale for detailed analysis.
