**Postmortem: Object Storage Tier Outage, October 26, 2024**

**Status:** Final
**Author:** SRE Core Team
**Date:** November 5, 2024

**1. Executive Summary**

On October 26, 2024, beginning at approximately 02:15 UTC, Northwind Cloud’s global object storage tier, “Glacier,” experienced a severe service disruption that lasted 14 hours, concluding at 16:15 UTC. The outage primarily affected write and read operations for a significant subset of our customers. Our investigation reveals that this was not the result of a single catastrophic failure, but rather a cascading event triggered by three distinct and independent faults. The initial fault was a mismanaged TLS certificate rotation that disrupted inter-region replication. This failure was compounded by a latent integer overflow bug in our metadata sharding logic, which became active only under the specific retry patterns generated by the primary fault. Finally, our incident response was significantly hampered by an outdated runbook that pointed responders to a non-authoritative monitoring dashboard, delaying diagnosis by over an hour. At its peak, approximately 40,000 tenants experienced API error rates exceeding 99%. We have determined that the window for potential data loss was minimal and contained to uncommitted writes that should have been captured by client-side retry logic. This document details the timeline of the event, the technical root causes, the customer impact, and our action plan to prevent a recurrence.

**2. Incident Timeline**

All times are in UTC.

*   **02:00:** A scheduled, semi-automated rotation of our internal TLS Certificate Authority (CA) is performed. The new CA bundle is pushed to services in our primary region, `us-east-1`.
*   **02:15:** The first alerts fire. Our primary API gateway in `us-east-1` reports a sharp increase in P99 latency and a corresponding spike in 5xx-level error codes for object storage requests.
*   **02:18:** The designated on-call SRE is paged and begins investigation. Following the documented runbook for "High API Error Rates," the engineer immediately consults the `Legacy-Primary-Dashboard`. The dashboard, a holdover from our v1 infrastructure, shows all core metrics for the object storage service in the green.
*   **02:25 - 03:10:** Believing the storage service itself to be healthy, the initial response team focuses on the network layer. This hypothesis is given credence by a coincidental, minor BGP routing flap reported by our transit provider around the same time. The team spends nearly an hour investigating potential network path issues between our API gateway and the public internet, a path that ultimately proves to be a red herring. Customer support tickets begin to accumulate, reporting widespread failures.
*   **03:15:** With customer reports directly contradicting the dashboard, the incident is escalated. The lead SRE for the storage team is brought online. He immediately questions the validity of the `Legacy-Primary-Dashboard`, recalling that it was slated for deprecation.
*   **03:20:** The response team locates the correct monitoring dashboard, `Object-Storage-V2-Overview`, which was deployed a month prior but had not been correctly referenced in the runbook. This new dashboard paints a completely different and alarming picture: the replication lag between `us-east-1` and our secondary region, `us-west-2`, is growing exponentially. The replication queue in `us-east-1` is completely full, meaning new writes are being rejected.
*   **03:45:** The team digs into the logs for the inter-region replication service. They are flooded with TLS handshake failure errors, originating from the `us-east-1` replicators attempting to connect to peers in `us-west-2`. The error message is clear: "certificate signed by unknown authority."
*   **04:30:** The root of the replication failure is confirmed. The replication fleet in `us-west-2` had not successfully loaded the new CA bundle that was rotated at 02:00. Connections from `us-east-1` services, presenting their new TLS certificates signed by the new CA, were being rejected by `us-west-2` services, which did not yet trust the new authority. This was our first root cause.
*   **05:00:** As a remediation, the team manually forces the `us-west-2` fleet to pull and load the new CA bundle.
*   **05:15:** Replication connectivity is restored. The massive backlog of queued data in `us-east-1` begins to be processed, and the replication lag starts to decrease. The incident appears to be on the path to resolution.
*   **05:45:** A second wave of alerts fires. This time, the metadata service in `us-east-1` is reporting critical failures. CPU and memory usage are skyrocketing on the metadata shard hosts, and client requests are timing out en masse. The system, instead of recovering, seems to be getting worse.
*   **06:00 - 09:00:** This begins the most complex phase of the investigation. The behavior is perplexing. The metadata service, which maps object keys to their physical storage location, is failing, but not uniformly. Only a specific subset of requests seems to be affected. The team initially suspects a "noisy neighbor" problem or a hardware failure on a specific shard. They also briefly chase another red herring involving a recent deployment of a new logging agent, speculating that it was consuming too many resources. This is eventually ruled out.
*   **09:30:** A breakthrough occurs. A junior engineer, while analyzing raw request logs, notices a pattern in the keys of the failing requests. They all have unusually long, complex string prefixes. Running these keys through a local testbed of the sharding algorithm, she discovers something extraordinary.
*   **10:15:** The second root cause is identified. Our metadata service uses a custom hashing function that maps a string key to a 32-bit unsigned integer. To determine the correct shard for a key, we perform a modulo operation: `shard_id = hash(key) % number_of_shards`. Due to a subtle oversight during its initial implementation five years ago, the intermediate result of the hash function could, for a very small set of inputs, approach the maximum value of a 32-bit integer. The massive spike in retry traffic from the replication service and external clients created a statistically significant number of these problematic keys. When the hash value was in the upper range, the calculation `hash(key) % number_of_shards` was being implicitly cast in a way that resulted in an integer overflow, producing a negative `shard_id`. Our shard router, not expecting a negative index, would enter an infinite loop trying to resolve the location, consuming CPU and never responding to the client.
*   **11:00:** The fix is clear but requires a coordinated deployment. A hot-patch is developed to change the relevant variables and function signatures to use 64-bit integers, providing more than enough headroom to prevent any overflow.
*   **12:30:** The patched metadata service code is deployed across all hosts in `us-east-1`.
*   **12:45:** The metadata service immediately stabilizes. CPU usage returns to normal levels. The API error rate begins a steep and steady decline.
*   **16:15:** Replication lag drops to zero. All queues are clear. The API error rate returns to nominal levels (<0.01%). The incident is declared resolved, 14 hours after the initial alerts.

**3. Root Causes**

*   **RC1: Flawed Certificate Rotation Process:** The process to rotate our internal CA was not robust. It allowed the new CA to become active in `us-east-1` before its trust chain was propagated to and verified by dependent services in all other regions, specifically `us-west-2`. This broke the chain of trust required for TLS handshakes, halting inter-region replication.
*   **RC2: Latent Integer Overflow in Metadata Sharding:** The core sharding logic for our metadata service contained a latent integer overflow vulnerability. This bug was dormant for years under normal traffic patterns but was triggered by the massive, concentrated retry storm that resulted from the replication failure. The overflow caused requests for certain keys to be routed to an invalid shard index, leading to resource exhaustion and timeouts.
*   **RC3: Stale Incident Response Documentation:** Our on-call runbook for a primary alert category was dangerously out of date. It directed the initial responders to a legacy, non-authoritative dashboard that masked the true state of the system. This misdirection led to a critical delay of over one hour in correctly identifying the incident's scope and nature.

**4. Customer and Business Impact**

*   **Service Unavailability:** The object storage API was effectively unavailable for a large portion of our customers for 14 hours. We have identified approximately 40,000 distinct customer tenants who issued API calls that failed during this window.
*   **Error Rate:** At the peak of the outage (from approximately 05:45 to 12:30 UTC), the API error rate for impacted services was over 99%.
*   **Data Integrity:** Our analysis indicates no persistent customer data loss. However, we have identified a 5-minute window (02:10 - 02:15 UTC) where a small number of write operations may have been acknowledged by the API gateway but failed to be committed to the replication queue before it filled. We bound this potential loss at fewer than 1,000 total objects across all tenants. Standard client-side SDKs would have received an error and should have retried these writes after the incident was resolved. We are proactively reaching out to the small number of customers who may have been affected.
*   **Reputational Impact:** The length and nature of this outage represent a significant blow to Northwind’s reputation as a reliable storage provider.

**5. Lessons Learned & Action Items**

This incident has exposed critical weaknesses in our technical architecture, operational procedures, and testing philosophy. We are committed to addressing them comprehensively.

*   **Lesson 1: Automation must be trustworthy.** A process like a CA rotation is too critical for semi-automated, unverified steps.
    *   **Action Item:** We will build a fully automated, closed-loop system for CA rotation. This system will include a pre-flight check phase that actively deploys a test certificate and verifies that every single dependent service in every region can validate its chain *before* the primary rotation is executed. (Tracked: NW-5421)
*   **Lesson 2: Every line of code can fail.** A single, subtle bug in a core algorithm, however unlikely to be triggered, represents a significant liability. We must program more defensively and test more creatively.
    *   **Action Item:** We will port the metadata service’s sharding logic to use 64-bit integers throughout. (Tracked: NW-5422)
    *   **Action Item:** We will develop a comprehensive fuzz testing suite for our core hashing, sharding, and routing algorithms to proactively discover edge cases like overflows and unexpected inputs. (Tracked: NW-5423)
    *   **Action Item:** We will implement more granular circuit breakers in the metadata service to automatically detect and isolate failing shard indexes, preventing a localized failure from causing a cascading service-wide outage. (Tracked: NW-5424)
*   **Lesson 3: Documentation is a feature.** Our operational documentation is as critical to our uptime as our code. It cannot be allowed to become stale.
    *   **Action Item:** We will establish a "docs-as-code" policy for all runbooks. They will be stored in a git repository, tied to service definitions, and subject to mandatory review and updates during any change to system architecture or monitoring. (Tracked: NW-5425)
    *   **Action Item:** We will programmatically link alerts directly to the specific runbook and dashboard for that component, removing any ambiguity for on-call responders. A quarterly audit will verify these links. (Tracked: NW-5426)

We deeply regret the impact this outage had on our customers. The cascade of failures points to a series of systemic issues that we are treating with the utmost seriousness. The action items outlined above represent our firm commitment to rebuilding trust and ensuring this class of failure does not happen again.
